About
The short version

My career has followed a fairly simple arc. Each time I got comfortable, I moved somewhere that would challenge me in a way the previous role could not. I started at Sanzyme, selling nutraceuticals and nephrology products in a new territory. That taught me the fundamentals: distributor networks, KOL relationships, and converting daily activity into actual revenue.

From there I moved to Pfizer, where I built a greenfield critical care territory from nothing. Then came Boehringer Ingelheim for cardiology, Infexn Laboratories for diagnostics, and eventually Toshbro Medicals for medical devices and ophthalmic technology. Each move taught me something the last one did not: product marketing, institutional sales, government procurement, channel development, and eventually full P&L ownership.

What ties it all together is a simple belief: the gap between a strong product and real commercial success almost always comes down to how well you build the go to market engine around it. Specifications, relationships, market access, pricing. These are the details where growth actually happens, and they are the details I care about most.

I am currently based in Mumbai and actively exploring marketing leadership, commercial strategy, or regional business development opportunities in the UAE.

Case Study 01
Toshbro Medicals
Scaling from $19.9M to $31.7M and Growing EBITDA to $10M
The Situation

By mid 2025, the commercial foundation at Toshbro was solid. The CRM was live, pipeline governance was in place, the channel partner network was expanding, and the automation layer was operational. But the business was entering a different phase. The easy gains had been captured. Revenue was at $19.9M, EBITDA at $6.4M (32% margin), and net profit at $4.2M. The next leg of growth would not come from doing more of the same. It would require a fundamental redesign of how we went to market with our two most important portfolios: Espansione (from Italy) and Moptim (from Certainn Technology, China), which together represented roughly 30 percent of total business. The challenge was no longer about building systems. It was about making those systems produce a step change in both revenue and profitability while maintaining the margin discipline we had built in the previous phase.

What I Did
  • Redesigned the go to market strategy for the Espansione portfolio. This meant rethinking everything from customer segmentation to pricing tiers to the clinical adoption playbook. The previous approach had been fairly uniform across geographies. I moved to a tiered strategy that matched product positioning to the purchasing power and clinical maturity of each market segment.
  • Took on the Moptim portfolio as an additional responsibility when we onboarded the principal in December 2024. This was a strategic bet on the Tier 2 and Tier 3 opportunity: Moptim offered excellent value for money products (OCTs, optical biometers, slit lamps, microscopes) that complemented our premium Heidelberg range. Within six months, we had outperformed Moptim's previous experience with any Indian distributor.
  • Built three KOLs for the Moptim brand who actively spoke about the products at clinical platforms and conferences, creating organic demand that reduced our dependence on direct sales effort alone.
  • Focused on EBITDA as much as on revenue. This meant renegotiating terms with principals, optimising logistics and service delivery costs, tightening discount governance, and ensuring that revenue growth translated into actual profit improvement. Growing the top line is one thing. Growing the bottom line alongside it requires a different kind of discipline.
  • Led the marketing team through a step change in ambition. We went from executing campaigns to owning commercial outcomes. Product positioning, clinical adoption programmes, KOL strategy, demand generation, and pipeline contribution all sat under the marketing function, and the team had to operate at a pace and standard that matched the growth targets.
  • Strengthened institutional sales and government tender pipelines. The DGAFMS win (covered separately) was part of this broader push, but beyond that single order, the institutional sales engine was contributing a growing share of revenue through hospital network partnerships and state level procurement channels.
Results
$19.9M to $31.7M
Revenue (+59.3% YoY)
$6.4M to $10M
EBITDA (31.5% Margin)
$4.2M to $6.8M

What I Learned

The $12M to $19.9M phase taught me how to build a commercial system. The $19.9M to $31.7M phase taught me how to lead one. The skills are genuinely different. Building requires problem solving, system design, and getting things off the ground. Scaling requires strategic clarity, team leadership, profit discipline, and the ability to say no to revenue that does not improve the business. The fact that net profit grew from $4.2M to $6.8M while maintaining a 21.5% net margin mattered more to me than the top line number, because it proved that the growth was structurally sound, not just bigger. Over the full three year period, the business went from $12M in revenue with $1.6M net profit to $31.7M with $6.8M net profit. That is the difference between growing a business and building one.

Case Study 02
Toshbro Medicals
Building a CRM Led Revenue Engine from Scratch
The Situation

When I joined Toshbro Medicals in late 2022, the business was doing $12M in annual revenue. Growth was happening, but nobody could explain the mechanics of why. There was no CRM, no structured pipeline, and no clear visibility into where leads came from, how they progressed, or why deals closed or fell through. The sales team, over 100 people across India, operated largely on personal relationships and ad hoc reporting. Marketing and sales were essentially separate functions that happened to coexist.

What I Did
  • Implemented Zoho CRM across the business. This was the company's first formal pipeline management system. The challenge was not the software; it was getting a large, dispersed sales team to actually use it consistently.
  • Designed a pipeline governance framework with clear stage definitions, conversion criteria, and weekly review cadences. This forced discipline into what had been an informal process.
  • Restructured marketing campaigns to feed directly into the CRM pipeline. Content, events, and KOL led demand generation were all tied to measurable lead flow for the first time.
  • Built distributor ecosystems and activated channel partnerships, including a cross border expansion into Nepal, creating new revenue channels that had not previously existed.
  • Introduced structured reporting dashboards that gave leadership real time visibility into pipeline health, conversion rates, and revenue forecasts.

What I Learned

The hardest part of building a commercial system is not choosing the technology. It is changing the habits of people who have been successful without one. If the people entering the data do not see value in it, the data will never be reliable.

Case Study 03
Toshbro Medicals
Winning the DGAFMS Tender: Rs.30 Crore ($3.6M) Defence Order
The Situation

I got word that the Director General Armed Forces Medical Services (DGAFMS) was planning to float a tender for retinal OCT devices for military hospitals across India. The standard market price for each Spectralis OCT sits around Rs.70 lakhs, but the tender was expected to be valued at Rs.1.5 crores per unit for 20 devices, a Rs.30 crore ($3.6M) opportunity. The space was dominated by established players with deep government relationships and years of defence procurement experience. I had none of that. I had never worked a defence tender before, did not understand the procurement process, and had no existing contacts in the DGAFMS ecosystem.

What I Did
  • Started from the ground up. Studied the defence procurement manual, understood the multi stage tender process, and identified exactly where in the chain decisions were actually influenced versus where they were merely formalised.
  • Mapped the stakeholder ecosystem meticulously. From the end user ophthalmologists in military hospitals to the procurement officers, technical evaluation committee members, and the key influencer who carried disproportionate weight in specification decisions.
  • Influenced the key decision maker through other channels and worked to get the specifications for Spectralis approved and included in the tender. When the OCT tender was eventually floated on the Government e Marketplace (GeM), the specifications were aligned to the Spectralis, which effectively levelled the playing field against incumbents whose products did not match.
  • Built relationships at every level of the decision chain. Not by pushing the product, but by demonstrating genuine clinical value through live demonstrations, reference site visits, and peer to peer clinical evidence sharing.
  • Navigated the full tender cycle: pre bid meetings, technical evaluation, clinical demonstrations, commercial negotiations. Each stage with its own stakeholders and its own dynamics.
Results
Rs.30 Cr / $3.6M
Total Order Value
20 Units
Spectralis OCT Placed
2x Market Price

What I Learned

Defence tenders are won before they are published. The real work happens in the months leading up to the formal process: understanding who writes the specifications, who influences the technical evaluation, and how clinical value is weighed against price. What surprised me most was that the established players had become complacent. They assumed their incumbency was enough. Coming in as an outsider with no assumptions actually turned out to be an advantage, because I did the homework they thought they did not need to do.

Case Study 04
Toshbro Medicals
Building a Tier 2 & 3 Channel Partner Network
The Situation

Toshbro's business was concentrated almost entirely in metro cities: Delhi, Mumbai, Bangalore, Chennai, Hyderabad, and Kolkata. The top tier hospital networks in these cities were well penetrated, but Tier 2 and Tier 3 markets (cities like Indore, Coimbatore, Lucknow, Jaipur, Bhubaneswar) were virtually untouched. The problem was not demand; hospitals in smaller cities were increasingly investing in advanced equipment. The problem was reach. We had 120 field staff including service technicians and a product basket of 20 plus devices. Our field force was stretched thin trying to cover everything. There was no bandwidth left to open new geographies, especially for the mid range GTM products like Catrhex 3 and Easy, which required volume driven sales rather than consultative high end engagement.

What I Did
  • Designed a structured channel partner programme from scratch. Not just a distribution agreement, but a full commercial framework with clear roles, responsibilities, and mutual accountability.
  • Moved the GTM products (Catrhex 3 and Easy) to channel partners. This freed up the existing sales reps to focus their time on higher end, consultative products where face to face engagement and clinical selling made the real difference. The reps still benefited from the channel sales because their territory targets and incentive structures remained connected to partner performance.
  • Built in strict information sharing clauses so that every client interaction (enquiry, demonstration, negotiation, purchase) was registered in our CRM. This was non negotiable: I was not willing to build a channel that gave us revenue but left us blind to the market.
  • Included territory performance clauses with minimum quarterly targets, ensuring partners stayed commercially active rather than sitting on exclusive territory rights without delivering results.
  • Retained direct ownership of the warranty and service relationship with end customers, meaning even channel sourced clients had a direct relationship with Toshbro, protecting the brand and ensuring quality of after sales support.
  • Recruited and onboarded partners through a structured evaluation process, prioritising those with existing hospital relationships in their regions over those with the largest distribution networks, because in MedTech, trust and clinical access matter more than logistics capacity.
Results

What I Learned

The biggest risk in channel partnerships is not that the partner will fail to sell. It is that they will succeed and you will lose visibility into your own market. The CRM registration clause was the single most important decision in this entire project. It meant that even though a partner sourced the lead, we knew exactly who the customer was, what they needed, and how the relationship was progressing. The other insight was that moving GTM products to channel did not just open new markets; it made the existing field force more productive on premium products. Everyone's incentives stayed aligned and nobody felt like they were losing out. That model (revenue through partners, relationships owned by us, field force freed up for high value work) is directly transferable to any market expansion, including the kind of distributor led structures common in UAE and broader MENA markets.

Case Study 05
Toshbro Medicals
AI Powered Sales and Marketing Automation at Scale
The Situation

By mid 2025, Toshbro's revenue had crossed $19.9M and the sales force had grown to over 120 people across India. But the marketing and sales operations were still running on manual processes. Lead assignment happened over email and WhatsApp, campaign tracking was inconsistent, and there was no automated follow up system. The CRM was in place from my earlier work, but it was being used as a data repository rather than as an active commercial tool. We were leaving growth on the table simply because of operational friction.

What I Did
  • Designed an automation architecture using Zoho CRM as the core, integrated with n8n for workflow automation and Zapier for connecting external marketing channels (web forms, email campaigns, event registrations) into the pipeline.
  • Built an AI powered post visit feedback system: after every sales rep visit, an automated agent triggered on email and WhatsApp asking the doctor for feedback on the interaction. The agent was trained on our product portfolio and competitive landscape, so it could intelligently answer clinical queries from the doctor in real time.
  • The feedback agent did more than collect responses. Based on the doctor's replies, it analysed behavioural patterns and provided the sales team with a psychological readiness brief before their next physical visit. It also automatically moved prospects through the sales funnel stages and alerted supervisors on progress and recommended next steps.
  • Built a Sales Tutor tool: a preparation system where any sales rep could plan an interaction with a specific prospect in advance. The rep would input the doctor's OPD size, consultation timings, setup type, and what devices were visible in the clinic. The AI then generated clinically relevant talking points and identified potential pain areas where our products could offer a solution.
  • Created real time dashboards that surfaced pipeline health, campaign performance, and individual rep activity metrics, giving both the field team and leadership the same single source of truth.
  • Trained the 120+ person sales force on the new workflows. The hardest part was not building the system but convincing a field team that technology would make their lives easier, not harder.

What I Learned

Marketing automation in a traditional sales organisation is not a technology project. It is a change management project. The technology was the easy part. The real work was designing workflows that felt natural to the field team, demonstrating quick wins early, and building trust that the system would help them sell more. The AI feedback agent and Sales Tutor worked because they gave reps something genuinely useful before every visit: context, preparation, and confidence. That is what adoption looks like when technology is built around how people actually work.

Case Study 06
Infexn Laboratories
Launching the OT Sterility Testing Vertical
The Situation

Infexn Laboratories had a strong diagnostics portfolio for fever, sepsis, and pneumonia panels, but the business was largely dependent on existing product lines and existing customer types. Growth was coming, but it was incremental. The question I kept asking was: what adjacent problem are hospitals dealing with that nobody in our category is solving? The answer turned out to be operation theatre sterility monitoring, a compliance requirement that most hospitals were handling manually and inconsistently, with no standardised diagnostic solution available.

What I Did
  • Identified the gap through conversations with hospital infection control teams and OT administrators. This was not a market research project. It came from actually listening to what clinicians were frustrated about.
  • Developed the commercial case internally and convinced leadership to invest in building a formal product offering around OT sterility testing.
  • Designed the go to market strategy from scratch: pricing, positioning, target customer segmentation, and the sales pitch itself.
  • Piloted the offering with a handful of progressive hospitals, used their adoption as case studies, and then expanded systematically.
  • Structured the business model around recurring testing contracts rather than one time device sales, ensuring predictable and high margin revenue.

Case Study 07

The Situation

When I took over the marketing function at Infexn, the company had a solid product portfolio but the commercial model was almost entirely passive. Revenue came mostly from referral based demand. There was no institutional sales programme, no government tender participation, and the relationship with large hospital networks was underdeveloped.   • Renegotiated supplier contracts across the procurement chain, achieving roughly 50 percent cost reductions without compromising product quality.
  • Designed clinical adoption campaigns targeting infectious disease specialists and microbiologists, the actual decision makers for diagnostic purchases in hospitals.

Case Study 08
Boehringer Ingelheim
Growing Cardiology Revenue and Shifting National Market Share
The Situation

I joined Boehringer Ingelheim to manage the cardiology portfolio, specifically Pradaxa (an anticoagulant) and Metalyse (a thrombolytic), across a large multi city territory. The territory was not performing to its potential. Cardiologists in the region were either loyal to competing brands or sceptical of switching. More importantly, the broader Pradaxa market had a fundamental problem: competing anticoagulants did not have a reversal agent, and neither did Pradaxa in practical terms because the reversal agent, Praxbind, cost Rs.1 lakh per dose. No cardiologist wanted to prescribe a blood thinner where, in the event of an emergency surgery or a serious accident, there was no affordable or quickly accessible way to reverse it.

What I Did
  • Mapped every high prescribing cardiologist in the territory and prioritised them by influence and volume potential, then built a structured engagement plan for each.
  • Designed and implemented the Pradaxa Patient Assistance Program. The core idea was simple but powerful: if any patient on Pradaxa needed the reversal agent Praxbind, whether for a planned surgery or an emergency accident, we would make it available free of cost and deliver it to any location within one hour. This eliminated the single biggest objection cardiologists had.
  • Worked closely with hospital emergency departments and pharmacy teams to set up the logistics for rapid Praxbind delivery, making it a genuine clinical safety net rather than just a marketing promise.
  • Focused heavily on clinical evidence sharing through small group meetings, one on one scientific discussions, and targeted CME events.
  • Maintained deep, trust based relationships with key opinion leaders, which generated peer to peer advocacy that no amount of detailing could replicate.
Results
$970K to $2.2M

What I Learned

The Patient Assistance Program taught me that the best commercial strategies are the ones that solve a genuine clinical problem. Cardiologists did not start prescribing Pradaxa because we gave them more data. They started prescribing it because we removed the one thing that kept them awake at night: what happens if my patient needs emergency surgery while on this drug? Once that fear was gone, the product essentially sold itself. The programme did not just work in my territory. It became a pan India model and shifted the national market share from 20 percent to nearly 60 percent.

Case Study 09
Boehringer Ingelheim
The COVID Digital Pivot: Keeping Revenue Alive
The Situation

In March 2020, hospital access for pharmaceutical representatives shut down overnight. For a business that depended entirely on face to face engagement with cardiologists, intensivists, and hospital procurement teams, this was not a mild inconvenience. It was an existential threat to revenue continuity. The primary mechanism for maintaining prescriber relationships, delivering clinical updates, and driving new prescriptions simply disappeared.

What I Did
  • Within two weeks, designed and deployed a fully digital engagement model. Not a generic webinar calendar, but a structured programme of virtual KOL interactions, one on one video consultations with high value prescribers, and digital clinical content delivery.
  • Segmented my physician base by digital receptiveness and tailored the approach accordingly. Some cardiologists responded well to webinars, others preferred WhatsApp based clinical discussions, others wanted curated research digests delivered by email.
  • Partnered with key opinion leaders to co host virtual CME sessions, which maintained scientific credibility while reaching a wider audience than in person events typically would.
  • Maintained weekly touchpoints with every key account through a combination of digital channels, ensuring that when hospital access eventually reopened, relationships were intact and prescribing momentum had not been lost.
Results

What I Learned

COVID proved that the value a good commercial person brings is not access to a hospital. It is the quality of the relationship they have built with the people inside it. The doctors who took my calls during lockdown were not doing it because of my company. They were doing it because we had built genuine professional trust over the preceding two years. That is not something a digital tool can create. It can only amplify what already exists.
